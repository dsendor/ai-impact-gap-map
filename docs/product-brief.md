## Why This Exists

This tool exists to help people answer two questions:
1. **How will AI impact what I care about?**
2. **What can I do to make that impact more good than bad?**

The MVP asks these questions at the biggest scale:

> ***Are we investing enough in the right places in AI to actually get good from it?***

AI is transforming the world faster than any previous technology. We have a narrow window to shape that transformation.

We made this mistake before. Many people believed the internet would democratize access to information and make the world better. Instead, we got a mental health crisis, deepening division, and concentrated power — not because the technology was inherently bad, but because **we assumed good would come rather than investing to ensure it.**

AI will have an even greater impact than the internet, smartphones, or the industrial revolution. The thing that most separates humanity from everything else in our universe is our intelligence — and now we're building machines that exceed us in key ways. The stakes for getting this right are unprecedented.

**We are repeating the same mistake.** Hundreds of billions of dollars are flowing into AI. Almost all of it is directed at commercial applications — productivity, entertainment, enterprise software. We are *assuming* AI will create good rather than *investing* to ensure it does.

This tool exists to change that: to identify **where resources — capital, talent, attention — would create the most good through AI**, and to make the case for redirecting investment toward those opportunities.

---

## The Problem

Funders and builders who want AI to improve the world face three problems:

1. **No map of opportunities.** Where are the highest-impact places to invest? Nobody has a structured, cross-domain answer.
2. **No way to compare.** Is AI in healthcare a better bet than AI in climate? There's no common framework for asking the question.
3. **Hype drowns signal.** Every AI application claims transformational impact. Without structured evidence assessment, capital flows to the loudest narrative, not the strongest opportunity.

And a fourth problem most people miss:
4. **We only look at nonprofits.** The framing of "AI for good" assumes good comes from the nonprofit sector. But a huge amount of potential good comes from the for-profit sector — healthcare AI, pharma, climate tech — and a huge amount of harm comes from for-profit externalities. We need to see both.

## What This Tool Does

An **impact gap map** for AI's impact on the world — showing where resources would create the most positive impact, how confident we should be, and what's blocking progress.

**Users interact at the Theory of Change level.** Each ToC is a complete impact pathway (e.g., "Diagnostics → Earlier Detection → Lives Saved") assessed on three dimensions: impact potential, evidence strength, and investment level. Individual causal claims exist underneath as the evidence layer — supporting proof, not the primary interface.

### 1. Holds benefits and harms in tension
This tool puts benefits and harms side by side with the same evidence standards. A funder can see that an AI application has strong benefit evidence *and* a serious equity risk, evaluated with equal rigor.

### 2. Surfaces the highest-impact investment opportunities
For each domain (healthcare, climate, education, labor, scientific research, and cross-domain), we map theories of change and assess evidence at each step. The result: a ranked view of **where capital, talent, or policy attention would do the most good**.

*Example: AI global health tools work on smartphones and could extend specialist-level care to billions. Evidence is strong for the technology but almost nonexistent for real-world outcomes in low-resource settings. This is a high-impact opportunity waiting for the right investment.*

### 3. Distinguishes what's ready to fund from what needs more evidence
Not every opportunity is ready for the same type of investment. Our gap classification tells funders exactly what action to take — from funding deployment to funding research to monitoring and revisiting.

### 4. Enables cross-domain prioritization
The core differentiator: comparing AI impact across healthcare, climate, education, labor, scientific research, and cross-domain opportunities **using the same framework**. *"Where should the next dollar go?"* becomes answerable.

### 5. Checks hype against evidence
Shows exactly where causal chains break down, preventing over-investment in areas where narrative outpaces evidence.

### 6. Sees for-profit and nonprofit together
Tracks for-profit and nonprofit impact together, because the goal is **net impact on the world**, not just what's labeled "for good."

## How We Build Confidence: Methodology

### Influences
- **GiveWell** — Cost-effectiveness analysis and causal rigor in charitable giving
- **J-PAL** — RCT methodology for development economics; their Project AI Evidence initiative is generating the first rigorous evaluations of AI-for-good programs
- **Social Programs That Work** — Top Tier / Near Top Tier / Suggestive Tier evidence classification
- **Cochrane GRADE** — International standard for healthcare evidence quality
- **3ie Evidence Gap Maps** — Visual methodology for showing where evidence exists and where gaps remain

### Evidence Strength

| Level | Definition | Equivalent in established frameworks |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Strong** | Replicated RCTs, large-scale empirical studies, or regulatory-grade data. Multiple independent sources confirm. | Social Programs That Work "Top Tier"; Cochrane GRADE "High" |
| **Moderate** | Single well-designed study, observational data with controls, or expert consensus backed by data. Plausible but not yet replicated. | Social Programs That Work "Near Top Tier"; GRADE "Moderate" |
| **Speculative** | Expert opinion, theoretical modeling, anecdotal reports, or corporate claims without independent verification. | Social Programs That Work "Suggestive Tier"; GRADE "Low/Very Low" |

### Time Horizon

| Level | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Established** | Impact already proven and observable today. | AI diagnostic tools already detecting cancer in clinical settings |
| **Near-term** | Impact expected within 1–2 years. | AI drug candidates entering Phase III trials |
| **Medium-term** | Impact expected within 3–10 years. | AI-driven climate modeling improving policy decisions |
| **Long-term** | Impact projected beyond 10 years; inherently more speculative. | AGI-assisted scientific breakthroughs |

### Gap Types

| Gap Type | What it means | What a funder does |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Needs Evidence** | The opportunity looks promising but lacks rigorous evidence. | Fund research, evaluation, or data collection |
| **Needs Capital** | Evidence supports the impact; what's missing is deployment capital, infrastructure, or scaling. | Fund implementation, pilots, or infrastructure |
| **Needs Time** | Investment has been made or could be made, but observable outcomes won't materialize for a while. | Monitor; plan for longer timeline |
| **Needs Evidence + Capital** | Both evidence generation and deployment investment are needed. | Fund evidence first, then implementation |
| **Needs Evidence + Time** | A study or evaluation is needed, but results won't be conclusive for years. | Fund research now; expect slow results |
| **Needs Capital + Time** | Deployment capital is needed, but impact won't manifest for years. | Fund deployment now; expect slow impact |
| **Needs Evidence + Capital + Time** | All three gaps are present. | Fund evidence and implementation; plan for long horizon |
| **Not a Gap** | This step has sufficient evidence and implementation. | No action needed |

## Causal DAGs: Finding Better Investments

Beyond linear theories of change, we use causal DAGs (directed acyclic graphs) to surface investment insights that a simple chain misses.

DAGs reveal three things that matter for investment decisions:
1. **Shared bottlenecks across chains** — A single investment that unblocks multiple impact pathways simultaneously. These are high-leverage opportunities.
2. **Hidden binding constraints** — Where the real bottleneck isn't where you'd expect.
3. **Conditional evidence** — Where "Strong" evidence at one step assumes conditions that haven't been tested.

### What each Theory of Change gets
- **Mermaid DAG** showing the causal chain + confounders with evidence-level annotations
- **Bottleneck analysis** — what's actually gating impact
- **Investment insight summary** — what the DAG reveals about where resources would have the most leverage

## MVP: What It Looks Like

**A cross-domain impact gap map** — showing where resources would create the most good, how confident we are, and what type of investment each opportunity needs.

### Two-layer data structure

**Layer 1: Theory of Change Summary (the product)**

| Field | Example |
|-------|---------|
| **Theory of Change** | Drug Discovery → Faster Development → More Treatments |
| **Domain** | Healthcare / Education / Climate-Energy / Labor / Scientific Research / Cross-Domain |
| **Type** | Benefit, Harm, or Mitigation |
| **Impact Scale** | Transformational / Large / Moderate / Small |
| **Weakest Evidence Level** | The weakest link in the chain: Strong / Moderate / Speculative / None |
| **Investment Level** | How much investment is flowing here today: Heavy / Moderate / Light / Minimal-None / Unknown |
| **Investment Case** | "Underfunded because evidence is strong but no implementation capital has followed" |
| **Primary Gap Type** | Needs Evidence / Needs Capital / Needs Time / combinations / Not a Gap |
| **Investable Insight** | What a funder should do |
| **Time Horizon** | Established / Near-term / Medium-term / Long-term |
| **Chain Narrative** | Narrative description of the full causal chain and where it breaks down |

The insight emerges from **contrasts across these dimensions**: a Transformational ToC with Strong evidence and Minimal investment is a screaming opportunity. A Large ToC with Speculative evidence and Heavy investment is hype.

**Layer 2: Causal Claims (the evidence backbone)**

| Field | Example |
|-------|---------|
| **Causal Claim** | AI-identified drug candidates succeed in clinical trials at higher rates |
| **Theory of Change** | Drug Discovery → Faster Development → More Treatments |
| **Step** | 2 (position in the chain) |
| **Evidence Level** | Strong / Moderate / Speculative / None |
| **Gap Type** | (8 options) |
| **Investment Level** | Heavy / Moderate / Light / Minimal-None / Unknown |
| **Key Evidence** | Brief description of the actual evidence supporting this claim |
| **Source** | Link to the best source for this evidence |

### MVP scope

**Healthcare (deep — ~7 benefit ToCs, plus harms and mitigations):**
1. Diagnostics → Earlier Detection → Lives Saved (Transformational)
2. Drug Discovery → Faster Development → More Treatments (Transformational)
3. Global Health → Extended Care → Reduced Inequality (Transformational)
4. Clinical Decision Support → Better Decisions → Better Outcomes (Large)
5. Admin Automation → Lower Costs → Greater Access (Large)
6. Genomics / Precision Medicine → Targeted Therapy → Better Outcomes (Large)
7. Mental Health AI → Broader Access → Reduced Suffering (Large)

**Additional domains (moderate depth — ~2-3 benefit ToCs each, plus harms and mitigations):**
- Climate/Energy
- Education
- Labor/Employment
- Scientific Research

**Cross-Domain (~5 benefit ToCs, plus harms and mitigations):**
- AI Operations → Nonprofit/NGO Effectiveness → Amplified Mission Impact
- AI Translation → Reduced Language Barriers → Global Knowledge Access
- AI Evidence Synthesis → Better Decision-Making → More Effective Resource Allocation
- AI Monitoring → Real-Time Accountability → Reduced Waste/Corruption
- AI Capacity Building → Smaller Orgs Compete → Democratized Impact
- *Harms:* AI Dependency → Institutional Deskilling; AI Optimization → Metric Gaming → Mission Drift
- *Mitigations:* AI Literacy Programs → Informed Adoption → Preserved Institutional Knowledge

### Views (on ToC Summary)
1. **Investment Opportunity Map** (board grouped by Investment Level) — scan for mismatches between impact potential and current investment
2. **Cross-Domain Comparison Table** (table grouped by Domain) — compare ToCs side by side across all 6 domains
3. **What's Ready to Fund** (filtered table) — Primary Gap Type = Needs Capital, Weakest Evidence Level = Strong or Moderate
4. **What Needs Evidence First** (filtered table) — Primary Gap Type = Needs Evidence, Impact Scale = Transformational or Large

## Long-Term Vision

The MVP answers two questions at the **systemic level**: where would large-scale investment do the most good, and what type of action does each opportunity need?

**The long-term vision is a personalized tool that helps anyone answer two questions:**
1. **How will AI impact what I care about?**
2. **What can I do to make that impact more good than bad?**

Theories of change are a **storytelling device**. They compile the stories that could happen — good and bad — show the likelihood of each, and connect them to concrete actions.

**Examples of personalized use:**
- A parent concerned about AI in education sees the possible futures — adaptive tutoring that closes gaps *and* AI dependency that widens them — with actions that shift the odds
- A professional worried about job displacement identifies the specific causal chain for their field and the steps that reduce their risk
- A volunteer with technical skills finds the domains where their contribution would have the most leverage

Over time, the map also becomes a way to **track whether AI is actually on a good trajectory** — as evidence strengthens, investments shift, and gaps close or widen.

## User Stories

**As a foundation program officer,** I want to see the highest-impact AI investment opportunities ranked by expected impact and confidence level, so I can direct funding where it will do the most good.

**As an impact investor,** I want to see where strong evidence exists but implementation capital hasn't followed, so I can spot underfunded opportunities.

**As someone exploring AI for good,** I want to see which domains have proven impact and where gaps exist, so I can figure out where my skills would be most useful.

**As a thought leader,** I want to cite specific, evidence-rated examples of AI benefits and harms, so my arguments are grounded in data rather than anecdotes.

## Success Criteria for MVP

1. **Investment clarity:** A funder can look at the theories of change and quickly identify the highest-value investment opportunities based on impact scale, evidence confidence, and gap type.
2. **Framework validation:** At least 3 independent reviewers confirm the structure is coherent and useful. Key target: Elie Hassenfeld (GiveWell CEO).
3. **Decision utility:** At least 2 funders say the tool shows them something they couldn't easily find elsewhere.
4. **Healthcare completeness:** All 7 healthcare theories of change fully mapped with evidence levels, gap types, impact estimates, and sources.
5. **Cross-Domain coverage:** At least 5 Cross-Domain benefit ToCs populated with claims, investment levels, and investment cases.
6. **Methodology credibility:** Evidence ratings hold up under scrutiny.

## Key Decisions

### 1. What are we analyzing?
**Unit of analysis: Theory of Change. Unit of evidence: causal claim.** Three distinct ledgers: Benefits, Harms, Mitigations.

### 2. Where does data come from?
**AI-driven, human-reviewed.** Claude gathers and structures data at scale; human reviews and scores.

### 3. How do we aggregate?
**Don't aggregate for MVP.** Show the data across domains and let users draw conclusions.
